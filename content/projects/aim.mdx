## The project

**AIM — AI-guided Computational Materials Design for Sustainable Manufacturing** is my
doctoral research project at the Leibniz Institute for Materials Engineering (IWT) and the
University of Bremen, in the group of Prof. Kabliman.

The core idea: materials design should be *fast like machine learning* and
*trustworthy like physics*. Pure black-box models can interpolate, but they cannot
explain — and in materials engineering, an unexplained prediction is a prediction
nobody will manufacture.

## Approach

The workflow couples three layers of physics:

1. **Electronic-structure concepts** provide the energetics that anchor everything above.
2. **CALPHAD thermodynamic modeling** turns those energetics into multicomponent phase
   equilibria — which phases form, at which temperatures, in which amounts.
3. **Kinetic simulations** add the time axis: how fast microstructures actually evolve
   under realistic processing schedules.

Machine learning wraps around this chain — not replacing the physics, but steering it:
proposing candidate compositions, learning surrogate models where full simulations are
too expensive, and keeping every prediction traceable back to a physical quantity.

## Why it matters

Sustainable manufacturing needs alloys designed for recyclability, reduced critical-element
content and energy-efficient processing. Physics-anchored AI workflows make it possible to
explore that design space orders of magnitude faster than experiment alone.
